waiter

After using various electronic ordering applications, whether free or paid, I found them to be complex and not very intuitive. Frequently, these applications come with tutorials to explain interactions, navigation, or features instead of being user-friendly.

Building on previous research of similar products, the design of this new proposal focused on achieving a clean and simple visuality that is intuitive and professional, without neglecting the wide range of functionalities needed for a waiter to take each customer's order.

Goals

• Serve more customers.
• Provide a faster and more efficient service.
• Decrease movement during service.
• Reduce the possibility of errors.
• Present a modern image for the business.

Timeline

February 2020, 4 weeks

This project is the outcome of the final assignment for the UX Design Fundamentals course offered by the California Institute of the Arts (CALARTS) on Coursera.

Tools

KEY FOCUS AREAS:

• What challenges or obstacles exist in using and adopting applications within the hospitality industry?


• What actions does a waiter take in the complete customer service process?


• What factors are crucial for a waiter while taking a customer's order?


What are the unmet needs in existing electronic ordering systems in the market?

TIMELINE

  • WEEK 1: Define a problem to address with an application. Identify potential users, their pain points, and goals. Define the objectives and functions of the application. Investigate currently available similar applications.
  • WEEK 2: Define the content map and interaction map.
  • WEEK 3: Create the main wireframes. Develop and refine the user interface.
  • WEEK 4: Create the interactive prototype.

MAIN CUSTOMER NEEDS

• Selecting quickly the products requested by the diner: Having the products displayed in categories and represented by photographs streamlines finding them and enhances their positional recall.

• Managing the entire order through the application: All the necessary information for the order should be automatically sent to the bar or kitchen, including specifications, addressing any errors or changes requested by the customer.

UI MOCKUPS

Allow adding specifications to the order 

Some applications of this kind implement order specifications with a text input. This method proves to be too slow during peak times. For this reason, I opted for using selectors with iconography for recurring modifications and an audio-to-text converter for more specific ones.




Product Filter by Allergens

Even though waiters typically have a good grasp
of the ingredients in each dish to provide accurate
warnings and advice to customers, a filter would
streamline this process.





Marking the dishes served at the table

Waiters often mark the dishes as they come out of the kitchen
on the order ticket to keep track of what is missing at a table
and request it if necessary. While waiters find this method
highly beneficial, applications of this nature often overlook it.





Notify about the dishes that have run out

On busy days, it's common for certain dishes
to run out, and due to the fast-paced work, communication between waitstaff and
the kitchen can sometimes break down.
Having the electronic ordering system alert about the sold-out dishes would prevent to ask the customer to repeat their order because what they requested is no longer available.






Freedom to manage the bill

Even when sharing a table, sometimes customers prefer to pay separately and request the bill individually, even at different times. There are also trusted customers who are allowed to leave the bill pending for payment on another day.
